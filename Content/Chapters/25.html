I met Toni in Boulder at a diner that was once a used car lot. The smell of diesel and bacon grease clung to everything: forks, plates, bacon. The staff smelled like eggs and axle grease and dirty dishwater. The seats sagged under truckers of indeterminable mass. One of them was Danbo Snail, who'd taken me on as his unloader and navigator and cigarette lighter on his long drive from Billings, Montana. I'd met up with him in Casper. He had a trailer to pick up that would get me to San Antonio, and from there he'd help get me to San Diego.
 
She came in and split the air, a wedge of vanilla, hammer of sage. A resounding crack of Jasmine. The bacon in my mouth transubstantiated into salted pus, which I spit out into my napkin, and I headed outside for a smoke. Half-way through my pall mall one hundred she stepped out. I heard the door open, but her smell made me ashamed of my cigarette, and I tossed it aside. She stepped close to my arm, leaned her head to within a hair of my shoulder and told me I was orange and grey and purple. Told me I needed to go north and west, needed to find my way by a river.
 
She half turned away. "I'm Toni. I'll see you again." With a step towards the door, she tossed a bracelet over her shoulder. I caught it, and there it was, a horse silver bracelet, two small beads to either side of the clasp.
 
It would've been easy to call her crazy. I couldn't shake the scent. I couldn't stop starring at the bracelet. There was something there, long back, and I wanted to trust in trusting. I told Danbo I'd unload him in Centennial and help him hitch up to his San Antonio trailer in Lakewood, and then I'd be on my way. He handed me two hundred and nineteen dollars, half a carton of smokes, and a business card in case I was ever close enough to lend a hand again. He left me at a truck stop just outside of Lakewood. I waited for dark and took off on foot following 119.
 
It would’ve been easy to call her crazy, but I wanted a direction, so I let the sage wind push me. Let the vanilla clouds comfort me. Let the Jasmine whisper gospel in my veins. I'd start over by a river north of here.

Sofie shows up with her nonchalant essential oils and hairy legs. She plops down with her beaded necklaces crashing. “So, no camp. Dead guy. Feeling desperate. Was I your first call?”
 
“No. Third.”
 
I toy with the silver horse bracelet in my pocket.
 

She shakes her head and pulls out a cigarette rolled in brown, unbleached paper. I hold out a lighter, but she uses her own match. “Alright, I didn't think this was a good idea, but if you get me a coffee and a bagel I'll listen."
 

I take a moment to gather myself. She looks good, smells good. "Alright, so, you know that thing in Eugene me and Dave and the rest were all in on?"
 

A nod.
 

"Alright, so, my share is still there, in the camp, along with the odd job and panning money."
 

She takes a drag and lets the smoke slide out of her mouth instead of inhaling. That's different. He stares at her lips. Thinner maybe?
 

"Alright, so, that comes out to twenty six thousand nine hundred seventy six dollars and twelve cents.”
 

 

Sofie looks like someone punched her. Mouth open, confused. Then she giggles. “You had me Elliot.” She doesn't even wait for the coffee and bagel, she starts to grab her bag and stand up.
 

 

No really, I have twenty six thousand nine hundred seventy six dollars and twelve cents, but maybe I shouldn't have said anything. “You’re right, I’m screwing around.” I say.
 

Sofie stands, looks at me for a few moments and snubs her cigarette. There's a twitch, maybe it's a shadow of a bird, but she sits down and leans close. “Wait. Son of a bitch. You really do don’t you? Where. Where the fuck is it?” I smell the tobacco and pear wafting up from her chest.
 

“At the camp.”
 

"No shit."
 

I shake my head. “Look, with the cops at the bridge and on the river they'll be suspicious of me."
 

She rolls her eyes, then looks around quickly, "you didn't have anything to..."
 

"No." I answer. I take a deep breath, "I just need you to go for a quick jog on the trail, go to the fire pit, walk six paces toward the bridge, then turn away from the river, there's a big bush, behind it, a rock, under the rock should be loose soil and a bag with my haul. I'll wait right here and we can figure out what we do next alright?"

I probably should have expected that would be the last time I saw Sofie.
 

 

I look down at the bracelet and close my eyes, and smell the jasmine and vanilla. I hold on to a baby sometimes too, that someday Toni or Sofie or Laura or Anne-Marie or Rebecca will come around and give me a shot again, that I’ll hit some big money or stop chasing my nose down to the water. How real is baby Hope?
 
Real enough anyway.
