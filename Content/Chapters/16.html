By Psuedonym: Cyrus I. Larsen

Blood traces a thin red line, stretching from brow to chin.
 
“Evelyn,” he says. “I’m sorry. I’m so, so sorry. You know how I can be.”
 
 She rolls over silently, hoping the room will stop spinning.
 
Her ribs hurt from Jake’s kick to her side when she was already down.
 
She doesn’t speak; she wonders instead how much the children saw. How much they heard? Hopefully, they don’t ask her what their dad meant when he was yelling you fucking cunt! and bruising her body and breaking her mind. How could she ever answer?
 
She will have to clean up the mess, of course. The table is askew. The red flashlight he threw at her is shattered everywhere— a fine symbol of her broken dignity. Her dehumanization and humiliation at the hands and fists of the one who once again broke his vows to have and to hold.
 
“Did the kids see?” she asked Jake gently, trying not to rouse his anger. He was still standing close. Subconsciously, she is aware of her new ability to measure striking distance.
 
“I hope they didn’t see the Christmas presents sitting on Jonathan’s bed,” he said absently, not really responding to her question. “You’ll have to return them if they did.”
 
“Okay” was all she said, slowly making her way to the kitchen sink and letting the faucet run. The blood was drying quickly. At least its stain won’t show of the deep brown carpet.
 
She thanked God she didn’t have to go to work tomorrow. It’s not that she would have made excuses anyway.
 
Everyone knew Jake beat her; no one did anything about it.
 
Jake entered his daughter’s room silently.
 
The children sat huddled on the bed— flattened like paint against the wall.
 
The bastard, Jonathan thought. He had learned that word from his father a week before and had been scolded for using it. He’s going to attempt to apologize. No, not an apology. He doesn’t have the decency. He’ll offer an explanation.
 
“I was angry. You know how my temper can be when I’m angry.” Jake looked everywhere but in his children’s eyes. He often mistook fear for respect.
 
He wants us to understand, to accept this flaw of character as reasonable and acceptable.
 
He hugs us goodnight. Nothing like a father’s protecting embrace, it was like hugging a fetid rat. His arrogance stinks. Sickeningly, nauseatingly, it lingers in the room even after he left. It claws at the walls, leaving marks and abusive memories paint cannot conceal.
 
Shelly and I sit quietly for a while.
 
Neither of us had even reached the age of ten. She was only four years older than me and I hadn’t even seen the first day of elementary school.
 
Neither of us knew what to do, or even if our mom was alive.
 
The second to last thing I saw while looking for a place to hide from daddy’s hands was the kick to my mother’s rib cage. The final thing was the remote control truck sitting unwrapped on the bed in my room.
 
I knew I’d have to open the tainted present tomorrow; I’d have to play with it and pretend everything is okay.
 
I also knew, above all else, that I’d become a better man than him.
 
That night I promised my sister— and later my mom when we were alone in the car— that if he ever hurt my family again, I will hold his life as recompense.
 
The promise holds.
