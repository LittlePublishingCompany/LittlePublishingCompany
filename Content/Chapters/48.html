The Ghetto February 3, 1943
 
            She said, “That is your privilege”, as she began to cry.
            You are so lovely when you cry. What nonsense. Two days before she died.
            She‘s the only one that knows I’m not crazy, but still she wants to argue it. I don’t want to give our bread crust to the kids. It may be the last we ever get.
 
            I’m still a loner, though the white cat follows me. I never see him come or go. He just is.
            I found a group of fighters on the other side of the city near the graveyards. Adrianna’s brother is with them. His name is Christov. He blames me for her death. His hatred makes me want to continue my solitude.
            I find I still need people for certain things. I ignore Christov’s hatred for the moment. I fear he will level me. I guess living is just not hard enough.
            Near the group is a huge shambled building that looks of no importance and that is constantly abandoned. It is anything but. It is Warsaw’s secret hospital. Nowhere else is war better understood and nowhere is war more horrible than hospitals. I must go because my cold feels as though it is becoming something worse. I have been coughing up blood lately.
            The garden does not want me but I fear it will have me yet.
            I enter into the building from the back. I am emerged into a world of misery.
            The daughters of the garden are crying tonight as their sons bleed onto the tables and die on their beds. The daughters weep so loudly.
            I walk past a table where a man lies silently staring at me as he holds his guts from spilling out onto the ground. Blood inches out of his mouth as he stares. I look deep into his eyes as they close and open no more. His hand falls away and so does his viscera, but they do not touch the ground. A nurse runs by and sees him. She turns away, and I see a tear escape her eye.  Somewhere someone prays. I do not know to whom.
            I do not want to disturb the nurse further, but as her back is turned I lightly grab her arm and she spins around. She is no longer crying; the faucet turned off.
           
You will have to go elsewhere if you want a doctor.  We are all that is here.” She points to the women hustling about taking care of the dying. I don’t know what to say, but I see how the daughters of the garden are diligent even in the face of death.
            “What do you need?” She asks earnestly as she can.
            “I am very sick… Coughing up blood. I need medication.”
            “I have nothing for you here, but if you’re coughing up blood and you stay out in this weather, you will soon die. Even if I had the medications, I do not know if I can help you.” She says.
            “What kind of medicine do I need?”
            “Some sort of antibiotics.”
            “And where can I find those?” I ask with some hope.
            “The hospitals here are destroyed or looted.” She stops there as if to think and then says, “But there is an asylum in Bydgoszcz. It would have the medicine. I do not know if it has been looted, but the town has been largely untouched by the invasion. You may have luck. I do not know, but it is worth a try.”
            Bydgoszcz is far away, at least on foot.
            She nods indicating that our conversation is over. I nod back. The smell of the hospital has now hit me and I feel a lurch in my stomach.  It turns violently as if to say you need to leave lest you stay forever. I abide.
            I pass by Christov again, still ignoring him, though I feel his eyes upon me.
            The miles are lonelier.
            Could I go back I would have happily given away our bread, not to please Adrianna but because it was right. But on this journey there is no more bread. There is only the garden.
