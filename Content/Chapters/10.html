    I take his rifle, registering one in the chamber, and creep through the den. The others have gone and left their rifles too. My library is missing. The shelves bare except dust.

    Outside, Nazis swarm the streets. Stacked books high in their arms, hiding torsos and faces. I duck back inside, slow my breathing. I rasp, “Freedom is death.” Where did that come from?

    It’s cold out. I better get a jacket. Shoes. Pajamas, slippers won’t last. Before I reach the den, soldiers again enter, glass screaming underneath their heels.

    Run. The morning sun is pale red. I dive between my house and the holly bushes: they’re welcoming as a chemo drip. Nazis march like ants house to house. Cries strangled in gunfire. Soldiers carrying books and newspapers and magazines.

    I crawl to another bush. The holly cuts blood lines into my back. I glass Nazis through the Mauser’s scope. At the intersection of Toulouse and Bourbon, I see the accumulation. Books heaved up like a great siege machine. I vomit. The holly stabs my back as it lurches up.

 

    Throughout the day it grows, that Great Tower of Wrathful Pagan Knowledge. Looms over buildings, demands tribute of them.

    My feet ache. The chill has grown. My teeth quake. I need a cigarette.

    Through my scope, there are no soldiers. I turn back, making sure they’ve not found me. Where is my home? When did I last feel it? My lot is only grass and the withering rosebushes Simone planted.

 

    I search the cobbled streets. The storefronts are boarded, the doors jammed. What happened to our homes? Uniform pink, three bedrooms, and white fences are now barren stone and dead lights.

    Faintly rising through the corridors, I hear marching. I run down Toulouse and keep running, run until I can’t breathe and my lungs rebel at the acid that fills them. The cold is deep here. The air thick, opaque. I choke on its heavy particles. I turn back, as the cold chases me toward that Great Tower of Wrathful Pagan Knowledge. Mere feet from it, I hide behind a daiquiris cart.     Nazis surround The Tower, worshiping its summit. Riding atop a war elephant, Himmler watches his troops. His shriek piercing, “Surrender what it wants!”

    The soldiers dance like aboriginal-wild men, exacting the ritual. The Tower is not moved. It demands devotion, hunger. Himmler cries, “More! More!” The soldiers dance brutally, whooping at the sky.

    A Panzer rolls down Bourbon, effacing the brick facade. The soldiers hush and fall on their faces. The elephant bows, and Himmler dismounts from the great beast’s skull. The tank stops and a form emerges from its canopy.

    Dear god, it’s him. Hitler. He salutes and dismounts. I bring the Mauser’s scope to my eye. Its not French but similar to what I used in the First War. I haven’t shot since those muddy ditches. I don’t know if I can hit him.

    I steady my sight, breath. He listens to Himmler’s instruction and nods. Himmler pulls his Luger, fires. On the bricks, a pool of blood and skull fragments gather. I drop my rifle and see my fallen enemy. Despite myself, I weep for him. No one deserves to die in this land.

    Himmler orders Hitler cast upon The Tower, and it erupts with flame. It infernos fiercely, warmly—whispers to me. The soldiers yarp primordial syllables that are deep and old and dead.

    Like droving moths, the inferno calls them. They enter it with chests open wide and screaming. The Tower grows with each consumption, reaching the heights of Babel. The building tops melt as wax. I stumble away from the daiquiris cart as it too joins the fire.

    Only Himmler and his proud beast remain.

    Himmler shoots the beast. Crimson punctures mingle down its filthy hair. Again Himmler fires, and the beast herds into the inferno. The Tower explodes, its flames now licking heaven’s crystal ladder. Himmler approaches The Tower like a martyr with arms outstretched.

    The Tower hisses.

    Himmler screams, “Why!” The Tower, now an old god, stands proud. Silent. Mumbling lost words just beyond earshot, Himmler levels the Luger at his temple, pulls the trigger. His blood pools with Hitler’s, and both are furnaced into a cruel ceramic amongst the bricks.

    I draw to Himmler’s corpse, searching its pockets, finding nothing except a copy of my book. I throw it to the fire, but it is not consumed. I creep to it, raising my arm to resist the heat, but the inferno grows no warmer the closer I am. I pick the book from The Tower’s care and am not burned.
