Warsaw, Poland (outside of the ghetto)
January 17, 1943
 
I’ll wait to see how this ends.
 
            Parents are gone. Mom went in some train cart without a fuss and Dad’s bones are still propped on the side of what’s left of the Prudential Building. Sometimes I go and talk to him when the night patrols transition. It’s cold right now and the snow buries him, so I have to wait for the wind to blow it away or else I’d get killed trying to dust him off.  He’s still smiling.
            I haven’t seen Mom since and I don’t know where they took her, but the stories the others tell aren’t good, so I said my goodbye.
            My sister Alicja was pushed off the roof of our flat; I tried to bury her, but I was shot at before I could and had to watch the Germans use her body as target practice until there was nothing left. She was six.
            My older sister Halina’s death was worse. The Germans passed her around then put a bullet in her head, leaving her body where it lay in our flat. I cannot bring myself to go back there. She was nineteen.
            That was a year ago.
            I’m showing you the bad, the ugly; it’s the way the sun doesn’t shine on us. It’s why the garden lies in ruins. Just remember there is no space between you and I, there is only death.  You’ll do well to remember that.
            I am alone at sixteen.
